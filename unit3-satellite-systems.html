Mechanical Subsystems
Structure · Propulsion · Thermal · Deployment Mechanisms
What is a mainframe subsystem? Every satellite is a collection of interconnected subsystems. The mechanical subsystems form the physical "body" of the spacecraft — they hold everything together, move it, keep it at the right temperature, and unfold it once in orbit.
🏗️ Structure
The structural subsystem is the skeleton of the satellite. It must survive the vibration and acoustic loads during launch, and then maintain dimensional stability in the vacuum of space.

Key requirements: High stiffness-to-mass ratio, thermal stability, electromagnetic compatibility.

Common materials: Aluminium alloys (6061, 7075), CFRP (Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer), titanium for brackets.

Types of structures: Box/panel structure (CubeSats), monocoque (tube-like shell), truss structure (large platforms).
Primary structure Secondary structure CFRP panels
🚀 Propulsion
Propulsion allows a satellite to change its orbit (ΔV manoeuvres), maintain its position (station-keeping), and de-orbit at end of life.

Types:
• Chemical: Monopropellant (hydrazine), Bipropellant (fuel + oxidiser). High thrust, short burn.
• Electric: Ion thrusters, Hall-effect thrusters. Very high Isp (efficiency), low thrust — ideal for small corrections.
• Cold gas: Compressed N₂ or CO₂. Simple, low cost, used in CubeSats.

Key term — Specific Impulse (Isp): How efficiently a propellant is used. Higher Isp = less propellant needed.
Monopropellant Bipropellant Ion thruster
🌡️ Thermal Control
Space has extreme temperature swings — a satellite can go from +120°C in sunlight to −150°C in eclipse.

Passive thermal control (no power needed):
• Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI) blankets
• Surface coatings (white paint, black anodise)
• Heat pipes

Active thermal control:
• Electric heaters (thermostat-controlled)
• Louvers (mechanical shutters)
• Cryocoolers (for IR sensors)

Goal: Keep all electronics and batteries within their operating temperature range.
MLI blankets Heat pipes Active heaters
🔧 Deployment Mechanisms
Satellites are compact during launch and deploy (unfold) once in orbit.

What gets deployed?
• Solar arrays (power generation)
• Antennas (communication)
• Booms (magnetometers, instruments)
• SAR/radar reflectors

Actuation methods:
• Pyrotechnic devices (explosive cutters, burn wires) — one-time use, very reliable
• Shape Memory Alloy (SMA) hinges
• Spring-loaded hinges (stored energy release)
• Motor-driven gimbals for pointing

One-time mechanisms must work perfectly — there's no repair crew in space.
Pyrotechnic SMA hinges Spring release
⚠️ Exam tip: Structural subsystem ≠ the whole satellite. Structure is just the load-bearing framework. Know the difference between primary structure (carries launch loads) and secondary structure (holds instruments, panels).

Electrical Subsystems Overview
Telemetry · Telecommand · Navigation · Mission Software · Control Electronics
The nervous system of the satellite. The electrical subsystems collect data (telemetry), receive instructions (telecommand), run the software, and execute the mission logic. Without these, a satellite is just metal in orbit.
Telemetry is the downlink of housekeeping data from the satellite to the ground station.

What does TM include?
  • Temperature readings from all subsystems
  • Battery voltage and current
  • Attitude (orientation) data
  • Memory and CPU usage
  • Payload data (images, science data)
  • Fault/anomaly flags

Process: Sensors → Onboard Computer → TM encoder → RF transmitter → Ground Station

TM frames follow standard formats like CCSDS (Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems).
Telecommand is the uplink of instructions from the ground to the satellite.

Types of commands:
  • Immediate commands: Execute now (e.g., turn on camera)
  • Time-tagged commands: Execute at a future time when satellite is out of contact
  • Stored command sequences: A script of actions loaded in advance

Security: TCs are authenticated to prevent spoofing (fake commands from adversaries). CCSDS TC standard defines authentication schemes.

Flow: Ground station → RF uplink → Receiver → Decoder → Command Management → Execution
The Onboard Computer (OBC) is the satellite's brain. Mission software runs on it to manage all subsystems autonomously.

Key functions of mission software:
  • FDIR — Fault Detection, Isolation and Recovery (automatically recovers from errors)
  • Mode management (safe mode, nominal mode, science mode)
  • Scheduling of operations
  • Data management and compression
  • Interfacing with ADCS, power, comms

Real-time OS (RTOS): Most satellites run an RTOS like VxWorks, RTEMS, or FreeRTOS because timing is critical.

Radiation hardening: Space processors must withstand Single Event Upsets (SEUs) — cosmic rays flipping bits. Solutions: ECC memory, triple redundancy (TMR), rad-hard chips.
Control Electronics refers to the hardware boards that interface between the OBC and the physical actuators/sensors.

Key boards:
  • Power Control and Distribution Unit (PCDU): Regulates power to all subsystems
  • Remote Interface Units (RIU): Distributed electronics that gather sensor data
  • Actuator Drive Electronics: Drive reaction wheels, magnetorquers, thrusters
  • Payload Electronics: Interface between OBC and payload instruments

Communication buses used: CAN bus, SpaceWire, MIL-STD-1553, I²C, UART.
✅ Key chain to remember:
Sensors → OBC (Mission Software) → Control Electronics → Actuators
Ground → TC uplink → OBC → Commands executed
Satellite → TM downlink → Ground Station → Analysis

Power Systems
Photovoltaic Generation · Solar Arrays · Batteries · Power Budget
Power is everything in space. Without power, no communication, no computation, no mission. The power subsystem must generate, store, regulate, and distribute electrical energy throughout the satellite's lifetime.
☀️ Photovoltaic Power Generation
Solar cells convert sunlight into electricity using the photovoltaic effect — photons knock electrons loose in a semiconductor (p-n junction).

Solar cell types used in space:
• Silicon (Si): ~14-16% efficiency. Older, cheaper.
• Triple-junction GaAs (GaInP/GaAs/Ge): ~28-32% efficiency. Standard for spacecraft today.
• Perovskite (emerging): Potential >33%, still maturing for space use.

In space: ~1361 W/m² solar irradiance (Solar Constant) at 1 AU. More power per unit area compared to Earth's surface (no atmosphere losses).
GaAs triple-junction 1361 W/m²
🔲 Solar Arrays
Solar arrays are panels of many solar cells wired in series/parallel to achieve the required voltage and current.

Types:
• Body-mounted: Cells on the satellite body. Simple, no deployment risk, but limited area.
• Deployed rigid panels: Hinged panels that unfold after launch. Most common.
• Flexible roll-out (ROSA): Rolls up compactly, deploys to large area. Used on ISS.
• Sun-tracking (gimballed): Rotate to always face the sun (1-axis or 2-axis).

Degradation: Solar cells lose ~1-3% efficiency per year due to radiation damage.
Body-mounted ROSA Gimballed
🔋 Batteries
Batteries store energy for use during eclipse (when the satellite is in Earth's shadow — no sunlight).

Battery Type Energy Density Cycle Life Use Case
Nickel-Cadmium (NiCd) ~30-35 Wh/kg 10,000+ Legacy satellites
Nickel-Hydrogen (NiH₂) ~55-60 Wh/kg 30,000+ GEO satellites (long life)
Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion) ~90-150 Wh/kg 3,000-5,000 Modern LEO/CubeSats

Depth of Discharge (DoD): How much of battery capacity is used. LEO satellites have many eclipses per day (~15 orbits) so batteries cycle frequently. Kept at 20-40% DoD to extend life.
⚡ Power Budget: Engineers calculate the total power consumption of all subsystems, then size the solar array and batteries to supply it with margin. A typical power budget has a 20-30% margin. Eclipse fraction must be considered — at 400km LEO, eclipse is ~35% of each orbit.
Power flow chain:
Solar Array → MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracker) → PCDU (Power Control & Distribution) → Battery (charge/discharge) → All Loads

ADCS — Attitude Determination & Control
Knowing where you're pointing · Controlling where you point
Why ADCS? A satellite must point correctly at all times — camera toward Earth, antenna toward ground station, solar arrays toward the sun. ADCS is the subsystem that determines orientation (attitude) and corrects it.
Component Type What it does Accuracy
Star Tracker Sensor Photographs star field; matches against catalog to determine attitude <1 arcsecond
Sun Sensor Sensor Detects direction to the Sun using photodetectors 0.1° – 1°
Earth Horizon Sensor Sensor Detects IR emission from Earth's limb for nadir pointing 0.05° – 0.1°
Magnetometer Sensor Measures Earth's magnetic field vector for attitude reference ~1° – 3°
GNSS Receiver Sensor/Nav GPS/GLONASS for position and sometimes attitude 1–10 m (position)
Gyroscopes (IMU) Sensor Measures angular rate — how fast satellite is rotating High rate accuracy
Reaction Wheels Actuator Spinning wheels — speed up/slow down to rotate satellite (conservation of momentum) Very fine pointing
Magnetorquers Actuator Electromagnetic coils that interact with Earth's magnetic field to produce torque Coarse control
Thrusters Actuator Use propellant to apply torque and momentum dumping Coarse to fine
⚠️ Momentum saturation: Reaction wheels can only spin so fast. When saturated, magnetorquers or thrusters perform "momentum dumping" — transferring angular momentum out of the wheels back into the environment.
🔄 ADCS Modes
Detumbling: After separation from rocket, satellite is spinning. Magnetorquers slow it down.

Sun acquisition: Point solar arrays toward sun to charge batteries.

Nadir pointing: Camera continuously faces Earth center.

Target tracking: Point at a specific ground target as satellite flies over.

Safe mode: Minimal power, sun-pointing, awaiting ground commands.
📐 Navigation (Orbit Determination)
Navigation determines where the satellite is (position + velocity), not just orientation.

Methods:
• GPS/GNSS onboard receiver — most common today for LEO
• Ground-based ranging — ground stations measure signal delay
• TLE (Two-Line Element sets) — pre-computed orbital parameters from NORAD
• Doppler tracking — frequency shift gives velocity

Navigation output feeds the propulsion system for orbit maintenance manoeuvres.

Communication & Sensors
RF links · Frequency bands · Onboard sensors
Communication subsystem provides the radio link between the satellite and Earth (or other spacecraft). It handles both telemetry/telecommand (housekeeping) and payload data downlink.
BandFrequencyUse
UHF/VHF300 MHz – 3 GHzTM/TC for small satellites, amateur
S-band2 – 4 GHzTM/TC, weather satellites
X-band8 – 12 GHzHigh-rate payload data (Earth observation)
Ku-band12 – 18 GHzBroadband, satellite TV
Ka-band26 – 40 GHzVery high rate, Starlink-type comms
Optical / Laser~1550 nmExtremely high bandwidth inter-satellite links

Higher frequency = more bandwidth but more rain attenuation and requires precise pointing.
A link budget is a calculation that determines if a radio signal will be strong enough at the receiver.

Key equation (simplified):
Received Power = Transmitted Power + Antenna Gains − Path Loss − Other losses

Path loss (Free Space): Signal spreads in space as inverse square of distance. LEO is ~800 km; GEO is ~36,000 km — GEO path loss is ~34 dB more.

Figure of Merit G/T: Receiver quality = antenna gain / system noise temperature. Higher G/T = better receiver.

Eb/N0: Energy per bit to noise density ratio — determines if bits are decoded correctly.
These are mission-specific sensors that collect science or observation data — not to be confused with ADCS sensors.

Earth observation:
  • Multispectral/Hyperspectral cameras (optical, NIR, SWIR bands)
  • Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) — works day/night, through clouds
  • Thermal IR imagers
Science missions:
  • Spectrometers (atmospheric composition)
  • Particle detectors (space weather)
  • Gravity gradiometers (GRACE mission)
  • AIS receivers (ship tracking)
  • ADS-B receivers (aircraft tracking)
Modern constellations (Starlink, OneWeb) use inter-satellite links to route data between satellites without always needing a ground station.

RF ISL: Ka-band, lower complexity.
Optical ISL (laser): Extremely high bandwidth, very precise pointing needed. Starlink v2 uses optical ISL.

Benefits: Global coverage, lower latency (signal stays in space longer rather than bouncing off ground).

Nano Propulsion · Testing · Simulation
Miniaturised propulsion for small satellites · Verification processes
⚛️ Nano Propulsion
For CubeSats and nanosatellites (1-10 kg), traditional propulsion is too large/heavy. Nano propulsion systems are miniaturised alternatives.

Types:
• Cold gas micro-thrusters: Compressed N₂/CO₂, simplest, low ΔV.
• Electrospray / FEEP: Ionic liquid electrostatically accelerated. Very precise, very low thrust.
• Micro resistojet: Heats propellant electrically to increase thrust. Water or butane.
• Solar sail: Radiation pressure from sunlight — no propellant! Very low acceleration over long time.
• Pulsed Plasma Thruster (PPT): Teflon ablated by spark, creates plasma jets.

Why it matters: CubeSats without propulsion are at the mercy of orbital decay. Nano propulsion enables controlled de-orbit and constellation maintenance.
Electrospray Solar sail Resistojet
🧪 Satellite Testing
Satellites cannot be repaired after launch. Testing must verify everything works before it leaves Earth.

Testing flow (AIT — Assembly, Integration, Testing):
• Unit-level testing: Each component individually
• Subsystem testing: Groupings (e.g., power + OBC)
• System-level testing: Full satellite assembled

Environmental tests:
• Vibration test: Reproduces launch vibrations on a shake table
• Acoustic test: Simulates rocket fairing sound pressure
• Thermal vacuum (TVAC): Chamber simulating space vacuum and temperature cycling
• EMC/EMI: Electromagnetic compatibility — no subsystem interferes with another
• Mass properties: Measure exact mass, CoM, moments of inertia
TVAC Vibration EMC
💻 Simulation
Simulation is used throughout the satellite lifecycle to predict behaviour before hardware is available and to verify software.

Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL):
Real flight hardware connected to a simulated space environment. The OBC gets simulated sensor inputs and drives real actuators. Closest to flight conditions without launching.
Software-in-the-Loop (SIL):
Flight software runs on a desktop computer against a simulated satellite model. Used early in development, no hardware needed.
Flat-Sat / Structural Model:
All electronics laid out flat on a bench. Full electrical interface testing without structural integration complexity.
Orbital Simulation Tools:
STK (Systems Tool Kit), GMAT, MATLAB/Simulink — simulate orbit, thermal environment, contact windows, power generation.

Deployment & Ejection Systems
How satellites get into orbit · CubeSat deployers · Launch integration
Getting the satellite from the rocket to its orbit safely is the job of deployment and ejection systems. This is the most critical one-time event of the satellite's life.
CubeSats (10 × 10 × 10 cm per unit, 1U) are deployed from standardised ejectors called deployers.

P-POD (Poly Picosatellite Orbital Deployer): Spring-loaded box. Up to 3U. Door opens → spring pushes satellite out.

ISIPOD: For 1U, 2U, 3U, 6U CubeSats. Used on many ISRO PSLV rideshare missions.

D-Orbit ION: A "space tug" that carries many CubeSats and deploys them at precise orbits.

Sequence after ejection:
  • Separation detected (switch on deployer opens)
  • OBC boots (30–60 seconds after ejection, UHF beacon starts)
  • Detumbling begins (magnetorquers)
  • Solar arrays deploy (if deployable)
  • Ground station acquires signal
For larger satellites (100 kg+), separation from the rocket upper stage uses:

Clamp bands (Marman rings): A metal ring that clamps satellite to adapter. A pyrotechnic bolt fires, ring releases, spring pushes satellite away. Extremely reliable.

Release nuts / Frangibolt: Shape Memory Alloy actuated bolts — heated by current, change shape and release.

Separation spring system: Ensures a clean separation velocity so satellite doesn't re-contact the rocket.

Launch Adapter: Interface structure between rocket and satellite. Transfers launch loads and includes separation mechanism.
Rideshare = multiple satellites sharing one rocket, each paying a fraction of launch cost. This revolutionised small satellite access to space.

Key rideshare providers:
  • SpaceX Transporter missions (Falcon 9 Rideshare) — as low as $5,500/kg to SSO
  • ISRO PSLV — popular for Indian and international smallsats
  • Rocket Lab Electron — dedicated small sat launch (~300 kg)
  • Exolaunch, D-Orbit — mission integration and deployment services

Key standard: ESPA ring (EELV Secondary Payload Adapter) — allows secondary payloads to piggyback on large rocket missions.
✅ Remember: Every deployment mechanism must work the very first time, in vacuum, at extreme temperature, after surviving violent vibrations. This is why pyrotechnic systems (explosive) are preferred — they are extremely reliable one-shot devices, unlike motors which can jam.

Space Entrepreneurship
New Space ecosystem · Business models · Startups to know
New Space vs. Old Space: Traditionally, space was dominated by governments (NASA, ESA, ISRO) and large defence contractors (Boeing, Lockheed). From 2010 onward, private companies began disrupting this with lower costs, faster iteration, and commercial models.
🏭 What Changed — Key Enablers
• CubeSat standard (1999): Standardised small satellites, enabled universities and startups to build affordable spacecraft.

• Reusable rockets (SpaceX): Falcon 9 first stage landing cut launch costs by ~10×.

• COTS components: Commercial Off-The-Shelf electronics (smartphones, FPGAs) now used in space.

• Cloud computing: Ground segment software moved to cloud, reducing infrastructure cost.

• Rideshare markets: No need to fund a dedicated rocket; book a slot for small payload.
💼 Space Business Models
Satellite-as-a-Service (SaaS): Sell data/imagery subscriptions (Planet Labs — daily Earth photos).

Launch services: Provide rockets for others (Rocket Lab, ISRO commercial arm).

Constellation broadband: Global internet via LEO satellites (Starlink, OneWeb).

On-orbit servicing: Refuelling, repairing, or relocating satellites (Northrop Grumman MEV).

In-Space Manufacturing: Microgravity for pharma, fibre optics, materials.

Space tourism: Blue Origin, SpaceX, Virgin Galactic.
🇮🇳 Indian Space Entrepreneurship
India's space sector was opened to private players with the creation of IN-SPACe (Indian National Space Promotion and Authorisation Centre) in 2020 and NewSpace India Limited (NSIL).

Notable Indian Space Startups:
Agnikul Cosmos
World's first single-piece 3D-printed rocket engine (Agnilet). Semi-cryogenic Agnibaan launch vehicle.
Skyroot Aerospace
Vikram-S — first privately developed Indian rocket launched (2022). Carbon fibre stages.
Pixxel
Hyperspectral Earth observation constellation. Sells analytics insights to agriculture, mining, environment sectors.
Dhruva Space
Satellite deployment services. Launched satellites on PSLV. Developing Thybolt smallsat bus.
Challenges for space startups: High capital expenditure, long development cycles (3–7 years to first launch), regulatory complexity (ITU spectrum coordination, export control ITAR/EAR), and competition from established players. But VC funding in space crossed $10B/year globally by 2023.
